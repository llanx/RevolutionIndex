- Foundational Works (pre-2000): Skocpol's States and Social Revolutions (France, Russia, China structural comparison), Brinton's Anatomy of Revolution (English, American, French, Russian), Goldstone's Revolution and Rebellion in the Early Modern World, Wickham-Crowley on Latin American revolutions, Foran on comparative revolution theory

**Key variable extraction:** Focus on extracting measurable preconditions, not narrative descriptions. "The French state was bankrupt" becomes "fiscal deficit as share of GDP" as a variable. "Elites were divided" becomes "elite factionalism" with proxy measurements.

Create a structured literature review of economic preconditions for political instability. Follow the document pattern from 02-RESEARCH.md (Pattern 1: Domain Literature Review Document).

**Structure the document with these sections:**
- Scope (boundaries: this domain covers economic theories and evidence linking economic conditions to political instability. Includes inequality, fiscal health, financial crises, and cost of living. Overlaps with Domain 1 where economic variables appear in revolution models, but this domain goes deeper into the economic mechanisms and evidence base)
- Foundational Works (pre-2000): Davies' J-curve (rising expectations dashed), Gurr's relative deprivation theory, Olson's Logic of Collective Action (economic incentives for mobilization), Muller's income inequality and political violence, Alesina and Perotti on income distribution and political instability
- Core Empirical Studies: Funke, Schularick & Trebesch 2016 (Going to Extremes -- financial crises -> far-right vote share, 20 countries, 800+ elections, 1870-2014), Mian, Sufi & Trebbi 2014 (financial crises -> political polarization), Piketty on inequality dynamics, Stiglitz on inequality and instability, Acemoglu and Robinson on economic origins of regime change
- Recent Developments (2020-2025): Post-COVID economic stress research, inflation and political attitudes, housing affordability as a political variable, wealth inequality trends
- Variables Discovered table: | Variable | Measurement/Proxy | Studies | Direction | Notes |
  Cover: income inequality (Gini, top 1%, labor share), unemployment, wage stagnation, cost of living/inflation, housing affordability, fiscal deficit, government debt, financial stress indicators, consumer confidence, real wage growth
- Key Debates and Contested Findings (e.g., absolute vs. relative deprivation, inequality as cause vs. symptom, whether financial crises always produce political extremism)
- US Applicability Assessment: This domain is highly applicable to the US. Most economic variables are directly measurable via FRED/BLS/BEA. Assess which variables have historically correlated with US political stress episodes (1968, 2008, 2020).
- Bibliography

**Citation rules:** Author-year inline, prefer well-known works, flag [UNVERIFIED], verify key citations via WebSearch, target 30-50 sources.

**Address Phase 1 Open Questions:**
- **#2:** Are there other applications of prospect theory to aggregate political risk? Search for Passarelli & Del Ponte (2020), Vis (2011), and other prospect theory applications in political behavior.
- **#3:** What is the empirical evidence for financial stress -> political mobilization transmission? Funke et al. (2016) is the anchor, but search for broader evidence on lag structures, transmission mechanisms, and conditions under which financial crises do vs. do not produce political instability.
- **#5 (partial):** How do other researchers handle normalization for trending macroeconomic series? Note any normalization approaches used in economic instability indices.

**US-first lens:** Economic variables are almost entirely applicable to the US. Focus on which economic indicators have the strongest predictive power for US political stress episodes specifically. Note: the US has never experienced hyperinflation or sovereign default, so variables like "hyperinflation" should be classified as "not applicable."
