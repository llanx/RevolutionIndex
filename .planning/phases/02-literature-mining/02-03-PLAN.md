**Structure the document with these sections:**
- Scope (boundaries: this is an adjacent domain covering how and when people mobilize collectively. The core revolution domains ask "what conditions produce instability?" -- this domain asks "what determines whether structural conditions translate into actual collective action?" Covers protest, social movements, civil resistance, and mobilization dynamics)
- Foundational Works (pre-2000): Olson's Logic of Collective Action, McCarthy and Zald's resource mobilization theory, McAdam's political process model, Tarrow's power in movement, Tilly's contentious politics framework, Snow and Benford's framing theory
- Core Empirical Studies: Chenoweth and Stephan's Why Civil Resistance Works (323 campaigns, participation thresholds, security force loyalty shifts), NAVCO dataset analyses, Beissinger's diffusion/contagion models, Kuran's preference falsification and revolutionary cascades, Lohmann's information cascade model, protest event analyses
- Recent Developments (2020-2025): BLM 2020 as largest protest movement in US history (Buchanan et al. 2020), COVID-era protest dynamics, digital mobilization, Chenoweth's 3.5% rule debate and updates, Mass Mobilization Project findings
- Variables Discovered table: | Variable | Measurement/Proxy | Studies | Direction | Notes |
  Focus on: protest frequency/size, organizational density (NGOs, unions, associations), participation rates, security force behavior, coalition breadth/diversity, nonviolent discipline, movement framing resonance, prior protest experience
- Key Debates and Contested Findings (e.g., 3.5% threshold validity, resource mobilization vs. grievance-driven models, online vs. offline mobilization effectiveness)
- US Applicability Assessment: This domain is directly applicable to the US. BLM, Tea Party, Occupy, Women's March, J6 are all empirically documented mobilization episodes. Variables like protest frequency, organizational density, and union membership are measurable. Less applicable: variables requiring event-coding of specific campaigns (e.g., NAVCO campaign-level coding)
- Bibliography

**Citation rules:** Author-year inline, prefer well-known works, flag [UNVERIFIED], verify key citations via WebSearch, target 20-50 sources.

**US-first lens:** Focus on variables that can be measured for the US over time. Protest frequency and size are measurable via ACLED/Count Love/Mass Mobilization Project data. Union density is available from BLS. Organizational density is harder to measure. The 3.5% participation threshold is interesting but operates at the campaign level, not as a continuous time-series predictor.

**Key variable focus:** Extract variables that bridge the "structural conditions" (from core domains) to "actual mobilization" (the outcome we're trying to predict). The most valuable variables from this domain are those that measure mobilization capacity, willingness, and recent history of collective action.

Create a structured literature review of media, information ecosystem, and political communication research as it relates to instability and collective action. Follow the document pattern from 02-RESEARCH.md (Pattern 1: Domain Literature Review Document).

**Structure the document with these sections:**
- Scope (boundaries: this is an adjacent domain covering how the media and information environment affects political attitudes, polarization, and mobilization. The core domains study structural conditions and outcomes; this domain studies the information channel through which structural conditions are perceived, amplified, or distorted)
- Foundational Works (pre-2000): Agenda-setting theory (McCombs and Shaw), media framing effects (Entman, Iyengar), spiral of silence (Noelle-Neumann), early political communication research
- Core Empirical Studies: Polarization measurement (McCarty, Poole, Rosenthal on congressional polarization; Iyengar et al. on affective polarization; Boxell et al. cross-country comparisons), media slant studies (Gentzkow and Shapiro), filter bubbles and echo chambers (Sunstein, Pariser, Bail et al. 2018 on exposure to opposing views increasing polarization), misinformation effects (Vosoughi et al. 2018 on false news spreading faster), social media and protest (Tufekci on Twitter and Tear Gas, Gonzalez-Bailon on protest cascades)
- Recent Developments (2020-2025): Post-2020 misinformation research, platform effects on polarization, algorithmic amplification studies, trust in media trends, AI-generated content concerns
- Variables Discovered table: | Variable | Measurement/Proxy | Studies | Direction | Notes |
  Focus on: media polarization (measured how?), affective polarization (feeling thermometers, Pew surveys), political news trust, misinformation exposure/belief, social media political engagement, information fragmentation/bubble measures, media concentration/ownership, conspiratorial thinking prevalence
- Key Debates and Contested Findings (e.g., whether social media actually increases polarization, filter bubble thesis vs. evidence of cross-cutting exposure, direction of causation between media consumption and attitudes)
- US Applicability Assessment: Highly applicable -- the US is often the primary subject of media polarization research. Most variables are measurable via Pew, Gallup, V-Dem media indicators, and academic datasets. Challenge: many measures are survey-based (not available from federal APIs) and may require `other-data` or `unknown` availability tags
- Bibliography

**Citation rules:** Author-year inline, prefer well-known works, flag [UNVERIFIED], verify key citations via WebSearch, target 20-50 sources.

**US-first lens:** The US is the most-studied case for media polarization. Focus on variables with established time-series data (congressional polarization scores, Pew polarization tracking, media trust surveys). Note that this is the domain most likely to produce variables tagged `other-data` or `unknown` rather than `fed-data`, since media/information variables are rarely captured in federal data systems.

**Measurement challenges:** Be explicit about which media/information variables have established quantitative measures vs. which are theoretically important but hard to operationalize. For example, "media polarization" has multiple competing measurement approaches, while "misinformation exposure" lacks a standard measurement.

**Note for downstream plans:** Variables from this domain may face the most difficult data sourcing in Phase 3. Be honest about measurement challenges while still cataloging theoretically important variables.
